Abstract

This project builds a multimodal retrieval-augmented generation system over behind-the-scenes photographs of my craft and prototyping practice — raw materials, tools mid-action, and finished objects. A quantized Gemma 3 4B model captions each image against a fixed schema (material, stage, action, people, composition, colour, detail); the captions are embedded and retrieved against natural-language questions, and a second, text-only Gemma model answers grounded only in what was retrieved. Compared against a SigLIP joint-embedding baseline on ten identical queries, the headline finding is a confidence inversion: on a query about a material my corpus doesn't clearly contain, the caption-mediated route (L2) returns a confident, wrong answer, while the raw-pixel baseline (L1) correctly signals uncertainty through visibly lower similarity scores. A captioning mistake, once written down, carries the same downstream confidence as a correct one.

Approach

Two routes, one archive

The system reuses the Level 1 SigLIP joint embedding as a baseline (retrieve_l1) and adds a captioning layer on top of it. A quantized google/gemma-3-4b-it (4-bit, T4 GPU) generates one structured caption per image, cached once to captions.json on Drive. Captions are embedded with all-MiniLM-L6-v2 and indexed with FAISS (retrieve_l2). At answer time, the top-k captions are handed — cleaned of bracket notation — to a text-only google/gemma-3-1b-it, which is instructed to answer only from what it is given.

Caption schema — and why

The schema is closed-vocabulary by design: free-text captioning invites the model to narrate rather than describe, and makes retrieval noisy because near-synonyms (e.g. "trimming" vs "cutting") fragment the embedding space. Constraining each field to a fixed set of options keeps captions comparable across the whole corpus and keeps the vocabulary of questions and captions aligned.

Mis-seeing dossier

Case 1 — Confidently wrong material identification (L2 inherits a captioning error)

Query: "close-up texture of plastic" · Images: the five results in Screenshot%202026-07-08%20171823.png

What Gemma said: Material field labelled these as plastic; the generated answer states "the images all describe close-ups of plastic objects with a predominantly white or grey colour scheme."

What is actually there: [confirm your material here — clay/plaster maquette? 3D-printed PLA?]

Why it happened: Plastic and other pale, smooth, matte materials share a very similar close-up visual signature — low colour saturation, soft specular highlight, no visible grain — and at macro scale the model loses the surrounding-scene context that would normally disambiguate material identity. Crucially, L1 on the identical query scored 2.37 at best (its lowest of any query in this atlas) — the raw pixel embedding correctly signals low confidence, while L2's caption-mediated route carries the same false certainty as a correct answer once the wrong label is committed to text.

Case 2 — Tool misidentification in a caption (drill described as saw)

Query: "hands drilling with a saw" · Image: Screenshot%202026-07-08%20170240.png (IMG_20240424_204157.jpg)

What Gemma said: "The image depicts a single person performing the action of cutting using a drill to create a hole through a wooden piece. The materials involved appear to be wood and a saw…"

What is actually there: A drill press making a hole in wood. There is no saw anywhere in the frame.

Why it happened: The caption schema's closed vocabulary for Action includes cutting, folding, painting, assembling, bending — no explicit "drilling" option — which may have pushed the model toward describing a drilling action using cutting/saw vocabulary borrowed from adjacent, more common captions in the corpus, rather than naming the tool it actually saw.

Case 3 — Answer truncation and a recurring nonsense trailing token

Queries: "warm red and orange tones," "messy workbench full of tools," "sanding a wooden surface," "hands drilling with a saw" · Images: Screenshot%202026-07-08%20172532.png

What Gemma said: Multiple answers cut off mid-sentence at the 120-token generation limit (e.g. "...tools scattered around it. It", "...vibrant colours. There"). Two separate answers also end with the same nonsense string glued directly onto an otherwise complete sentence, with no space: "...characteristic of a natural or weathered aesthetic.yetanow" and "...characteristic of the material itself.yetanow".

What is actually there: No visual content is being misdescribed here — this is a generation-side artifact, not a captioning error.

Why it happened: The plain truncation is simply max_new_tokens=120 being too low for some answers. The repeated "yetanow" is more interesting: it appears in exactly the same position both times, right after what reads as a natural closing sentence — consistent with no_repeat_ngram_size=3 (added to fix an earlier repetition-loop bug) blocking the model's preferred closing phrase and forcing it onto a low-probability filler token instead. A fix for one failure mode (repetition) introducing a smaller, different artifact is a pattern worth naming explicitly rather than hiding.

Reflection

What the machine kept getting wrong, and what that says

The most consistent failure across this project wasn't a single wrong caption — it was a pattern of compounding: each stage in the L2 pipeline trusts the stage before it completely, with no way to flag its own uncertainty forward. Gemma's VLM caption is treated as ground truth by the text embedder; the embedder's retrieval is treated as ground truth by the generator. When the VLM was confidently wrong about a material (Case 1), nothing downstream had a mechanism to catch it — the wrong label propagated with full confidence all the way to a generated sentence a reader would have no reason to doubt. The closed-vocabulary schema helped consistency across the corpus, but Case 2 shows it can also actively distort a caption when the real content (drilling) has no clean match in the fixed Action list — the model borrowed vocabulary from a more common category rather than admitting the mismatch.

The most useful practical lesson came from fixing the same generator bug twice. Adding repetition_penalty to stop a degenerate repetition loop first caused the model to code-switch into Arabic (over-penalizing common English tokens pushed probability mass elsewhere), and even after dialing that back, a milder version of the same mechanism likely produced the "yetanow" artifact in Case 3. Each fix was correct on its own terms and each introduced a smaller version of a related problem — which suggests that with another week, I would spend it on the generation stage rather than the schema: increasing max_new_tokens, and testing whether a different small model handles these constraints more gracefully than Gemma 3 1B does, rather than tuning penalty values further.

The clearest surprise was how directly the L1 vs. L2 comparison mapped onto a single mechanism: every query where the two routes disagreed was a query where the true answer was ambiguous or absent from the corpus, and in every one of those cases, L1's lower confidence score was the more trustworthy signal. That was not something I expected going in — I assumed captioning would at worst be neutral, not actively misleading.

Limitations
  • Single VLM pass per image, greedy decoding, no self-consistency check — captions are a single sample, not a consensus. Case 1 shows a wrong material label carries the same downstream confidence as a right one.
  • Closed-vocabulary schema trades recall of unusual content for consistency; Case 2 shows it can actively distort a caption when the true action (drilling) has no clean match in the fixed Action list.
  • The corpus contains at least one duplicate image indexed under two filenames (IMG_20220818_132246.jpg / IMG_20220818_132246 (1).jpg), which silently inflates apparent retrieval confidence for queries that happen to match it.
  • Generation is capped at 120 tokens, which visibly truncates some answers mid-sentence (Case 3); raising this limit was not done before submission due to time constraints.
